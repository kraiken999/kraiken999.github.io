Plastic pyrolysis – visual overview

In MAP and related pyrolysis systems, plastic is heated in a chamber without oxygen until it breaks down into gas and liquid products that can be condensed into usable fuel.

  • Dense plastic enters a pyrolysis chamber.
  • Chamber is heated to around 450 °C.
  • Plastic melts and produces gas.
  • Gas flows into a condenser where it cools and becomes liquid fuel.

What is plastoline?

Plastoline is a renewable, crude‑oil‑like fuel created by breaking down plastic waste through microwave‑assisted pyrolysis (MAP). It matters because it reframes plastic not as a permanent pollutant but as a valuable energy resource that can be reclaimed rather than discarded. Humanity has produced over 6.3 billion metric tons of plastic waste since the 1950s — the equivalent of 7.2 trillion grocery bags, enough to reach the moon and back 5,790 times. Plastoline offers a way to convert this overwhelming environmental burden into usable energy, potentially powered by solar panels, making it a bridge between waste management and clean energy.

MAP (Microwave-Assisted Pyrolysis): How it works

The basic idea

Microwave‑assisted pyrolysis (MAP) is a process that uses a combination of high heat — around 450 °C — and microwave radiation to break plastic down into its fundamental hydrocarbon molecules. Unlike traditional burning, pyrolysis occurs in a low‑oxygen environment, preventing combustion and instead causing the plastic to thermally decompose.

In MAP, microwaves accelerate this breakdown by energizing the plastic at the molecular level, allowing long polymer chains to split into smaller, fuel‑like components. The resulting gases and melted plastic are then condensed and distilled into crude oil, which can be refined into plastoline.

Step-by-step MAP process

  • Feed: Plastic waste is collected and prepared.
  • Microwave-assisted heating: Plastic is exposed to microwaves and high heat in a low-oxygen chamber.
  • Pyrolysis: Long polymer chains break into smaller hydrocarbon molecules.
  • Gas & liquid formation: The breakdown produces gases and liquid fractions.
  • Condensation & distillation: Gases are condensed and distilled into plastoline-like fuel.

Microplastics: How they affect the human body

Microplastics and nanoplastics are now found in the brain, heart, testicles, placenta, lymph nodes, stomach, urine, semen, breastmilk, and newborn meconium. Humans ingest or inhale an estimated 74,000–121,000 particles per year, not including nanoplastics, which are even more numerous.

Routes of exposure

There are three main routes through which the human body is exposed to micro- and nanoplastics (MNPs):

  • Inhalation: Breathing in airborne particles.
  • Ingestion: Consuming contaminated food and water.
  • Dermal contact: Skin exposure to particles.

Fertility & future generations

Additives like phthalates and BPA disrupt sperm development and epigenetic programming, affecting future generations. Microplastics are not just an environmental issue— they are a generational health issue.

Social & cultural change: A paradigm shift in energy

A major question arises: Does plastoline require us to keep producing plastic? In the short term, we already have far more plastic waste than we can handle, enough to sustain MAP systems for decades without producing a single new plastic item. The danger lies in the long‑term economic incentives: if plastoline becomes profitable, industries — especially big oil — may be tempted to continue producing plastic to maintain fuel supply.
